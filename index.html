Why this tutorial?

Reliable multimodal intelligence requires more than accurate answers.

Large Vision-Language Models extend LLMs to multimodal inputs and now support visual question answering, retrieval, code generation, mathematical reasoning, and other high-impact tasks.

Yet these systems can be overconfident, generate convincing but incorrect content, and remain vulnerable to adversarial manipulation. Such failures can spread misinformation and create serious risks in medical, financial, legal, autonomous, and security-sensitive deployments.

This tutorial connects three traditionally separate perspectives—hallucination analysis, uncertainty quantification, and adversarial robustness—into a single workflow for identifying failure modes, estimating predictive risk, and designing defenses.

Learning objectives

What participants will be able to do

Move from diagnosing model failures to evaluating and mitigating them in practical LLM and LVLM pipelines.

01

Deconstruct hallucinations

Distinguish failures rooted in training data, multimodal grounding, model bias, context, and decoding.

02

Understand uncertainty

Explain why token probability alone is insufficient and compare token-, sequence-, and semantic-level uncertainty.

03

Model the threat surface

Recognize prompt injection, jailbreaks, data poisoning, multimodal attacks, and discrete suffix optimization.

04

Evaluate defenses

Assess fact-checking loops, context enhancement, adversarial training, purification, and constrained decoding.

05

Build safety audits

Use practical metrics, open-source tools, and reproducible experiments to audit model reliability.

The unified framework

Three complementary pillars of LLM and LVLM safety

Part I Angela Yao

Hallucination & grounding

Understand when models contradict the source, external knowledge, the image, or the user's actual intent.

  • Intrinsic and extrinsic hallucinations
  • Bias and user-misconception following
  • Visual grounding and verification
  • Fact-checking and self-correction loops
Part II Gianni Franchi

Uncertainty quantification

Measure what the model does not know at token, sequence, semantic, and predictive levels.

  • Overconfidence on incorrect generations
  • Calibration and selective prediction
  • Bayesian methods and deep ensembles
  • Token entropy vs. semantic variance
Part III Mael Jenny

Adversarial attacks & defense

Map attack surfaces across prompts, visual inputs, context windows, tools, and fine-tuning pipelines.

  • Gradient-based and black-box attacks
  • Prompt injection and jailbreaks
  • Adversarial training and purification
  • Safety-constrained decoding

Audience & prerequisites

Designed for the computer vision and trustworthy-AI communities

The tutorial is intended for academic researchers, graduate students, machine-learning engineers, and industry practitioners working with foundation models.

Recommended background

  • Graduate-level deep learning and optimization
  • Familiarity with attention and autoregressive models
  • Basic Python and Hugging Face experience

No prior safety specialization required

Core concepts in LLMs, LVLMs, uncertainty, and attacks will be introduced progressively, with mathematical intuition and practical examples.

Diversity & inclusion

Multiple perspectives are essential to responsible AI safety.

The tutorial brings together academia and industry, Europe and Asia, and a broad range of career stages. This combination supports a balanced discussion of theoretical rigor, practical constraints, and the societal consequences of deploying generative models.

We are committed to an inclusive learning environment and to presenting safety methods in a way that is accessible to newcomers while remaining useful to experienced researchers and practitioners.

Positioning

Connecting topics usually taught in isolation

Prior tutorials have addressed uncertainty, hallucinations, or jailbreaks separately. This tutorial emphasizes their interaction in a shared risk-management framework.
